Place of Articulation

Consonants are determined primarily by two things: the place of articulation and manner of articulation

The place of articulation is where friction is made in the vocal tract for a given sound. Usually, this is determined by the
tongue, and where it makes contact to the rest of the mouth. However, there are places of articulation that act independently
of the tongue.

There are four major categories that the places of articulation are grouped into based on their phonetic similarities and
their location. These categories usually have one or more sounds that overlap between two categories:

Category Description
Labial Labial sounds are made with the lips. This includes labial and labiodental sounds.
Coronal Coronal sounds are made with the alveolar ridge, a bump located directly behind the teeth
This category includes dental, alveolar, palatoalveolar, retroflex and alveolopalatal consonants.
This category includes a subcategory called postalveolar consonants, which are made directly behind the
alveolar ridge, and includes retroflex, palatoalveolar and alveolopalatal.
Dorsal Dorsal sounds are made with the top of the mouth, with the hard and soft palates. This includes the
alveolopalatal, palatal, velar and uvular sounds.
Laryngeal Layngeal consonants are made either with the larynx (a.k.a the voicebox), or around it. This includes
pharyngeal, epiglottal and glottal consonants. Many also put uvular sounds in this category.

There are also common sounds which use the lips alongside a dorsal place of articulation. These include labio-velar
sounds that use the soft palate/velum and the lips, and labio-palatal sounds that use the hard palate and lips

Manner of Articulation

Manner of articulation is the other primary factor determining how consonants are made. This is often determined by how close
an articulator (usually the tongue) to the place of articulation. However, there are other more complicated parts that go into
articulation, which will be discussed.
For consonants, there are 4 primary groups of articulations:

  • Stops are made by blocking air from exiting the mouth completely, building up pressure and then releasing.
    The most simple category of stops are called plosives
  • Fricatives are made by touching the tongue to the place of articulation, but weakly enough to let air pass.
    This usually results in a hissy or breathy sound.
  • Trills are made by bringing the tip of the tongue to the place of articulation and relaxing it, allowing breath
    to make the tongue vibrate.
  • Approximants are made by bringing the tongue close to the place of articulation without touching it. These sounds
    are close to vowels due to the similarly lower friction.

Stops and fricatives are in a superclass called obstruents, where the sound is formed by significantly blocking airflow
Within these categories, there are subtypes:

  • Nasals are a variety of stops that allow air to flow through the nose but not the mouth.
  • Implosives are a variety of stops where the glottis moves down as air is blown out, partially trapping air as
    it is being blown out.
  • Ejectives are a variety of stops where the glottis is constricted, blocking airflow. Air is instead pushed ou
  • Clicks are a type of obstruent that is close to a stop. Air is trapped between a dorsal point of articulation and
    a different place infront of it, starting at the hard palate. Clicks can vary by both points of articulation.
  • Taps/flaps are sounds where the tongue only briefly touches the place of articulation. Taps and flaps are often typed
    together, but they are distinct by how the tongue moves to and from the place of articulation. To me, taps act more like a
    short stop, and flaps act more like a single flap of a trill. This wikipedia page is a good starting place to learn more.
  • Affricates are sounds that start as a plosive and ends as a fricative. Airflow is stopped, pressure is built, but
    the air is let out gradually as a fricative.
  • Lateral fricatives are fricatives where the airflow is split in two, having air go through either side of the
    tongue, but being blocked through the center. This is usually done by putting the sides of the back of the tongue against
    the molars while articulating a fricative.
  • Lateral affricates are the same as normal affricates, but the stop is released as a lateral fricative.
  • Lateral approximants are approximants where the airflow is split in two like a lateral fricative, but while
    an approximant is being made instead of a fricative.

Nearly every place of articulation has a stricture series, where the articulator is gradually lowered to produce different
sounds. Wikipedia has a good page on this.

Voicing and Phonation

Here's a fun question: what's the difference between P and B? They have the same place and manner of articulation, but they
are definitely for different sounds in English. The answer is voicing.

Voicing is done by vibrating the vocal cords. /p/ is voiceless, where the vocal chords don't vibrate. /b/ is voiced, where the
vocal cords do vibrate as the sound is made. You can confirm This by feeling your throat as you make /p/ and /b/

Voicing is one of many phonations. Another is called aspiration. Aspiration is adding extra breath to a consonant. In English,
voiceless plosives are aspirated at the beginning of words, and not aspirated at the end. It may be hard for English speakers
to hear the difference, but putting your hand close to your mouth as you say "pop" lets you feel the difference between the two.
Heavily voiced sounds are never aspirated, but one can make breathy voiced sounds, where an aspirated sound is partially voiced.
This is because both airflow and voicing is determined by the glottis, the opening between the two vocal cords. Voicing needs
the vibration of the two vocal cords, while aspiration needs them to be open and relaxed. Breathy voicing is essentially a
compromise between the two. English does not distinguish between them, but they can be made by trying to whisper a voiced sound
such as /b/. On the IPA consonant chart, voiced consonants are put to the right of a voiceless consonants.

A vocal fry, formally called creaky voicing, is another form of voicing where the vocal cords are compressed more. The
stereotypical "surfer dude" voice is notable for its use of creaky voicing.

Tone is another major type of phonation, that many languages use to distinguish sounds, usually vowels. Sounds are distinguished
in tone by changing the pitch of the sound, either gradually, or in quick steps. English uses a gradual tone when questions
are being asked, specifically the rising tone.<

Vowel Height

When the tongue goes lower than the point of being an approximant, sounds enter "vowel space". Tongue height is one of three primary
factors which determine what vowel is made.

/i/, as in bee, lies at the top of vowel space, while /a/ or /ɑ/ as in father, lies at the
bottom. Between the heights of /i/ and /a/ lie sounds such as /ɛ/ like is bet.

Vowel Depth

The depth of a vowel is the second primary factor in making a vowel. /i/ as in bee and /u/ as in you
are roughly at the same height, but are very different vowels. They differ in rounding, but also differ drastically in depth
/i/ is a front vowel, and /u/ is a back vowel. There are also central vowels, with /ə/ as in uh being the
most common one found in English. Furthermore, there are sounds that go between these 3 depths. /ɪ/ as in it
is near-front, between front and center. /ʊ/ as in could is near-back, between center and back.

Above I have described the convention in which the IPA describes all vowels, where they are in a 2D space, often represented by
a vowel trapezoid. However, this is not completely representative of how they are truly made. For better details see this
and this.

Rounding

As briefly mentioned in the section on vowel depth, vowels also differ in rounding. Rounded vowels have the lips more
closed, while unrounded vowels have the lips more open. /i/ and /u/ are very unrounded and very unrounded respectively, and thus
make for good examples in rounding. Every vowel can be rounded or unrounded. Many languages have vowel pairs only made distinct
by their roundedness. French is an example of this, having /i/ as in paris, and /y/ as in tu.

Suprasegmentals

Suprasegmentals are used to distinguish how sounds transition from one to the other.

The tie bar is a suprasegmental used primarily to represent affricates, such as /t͡ʃ/ as in chair. They are
also used in biarticulated sounds, where two, non interfering consonants are made at the same time

There are many other, primarily determining stress, sound length and pauses, which are documented on other pages from this site.

Diacritics

All that was said above is most of what is needed to distinguish between the "letters" of the IPA. However, some of what was
discussed above needs diacritics, also known as accent marks. Generalized English needs little to no diacritics to
transcribe, but they are regardless an important facet of the IPA. Many languages have sounds, such as nasal vowels, that can
only be represented with diacritcs. Other diacritics are often used to be more precise in transcribing specific sounds in a
language that does not otherwise care about the distinction. Because they are a complicated subject, they will be omitted
here, and they can instead be found elsewhere on the site.
